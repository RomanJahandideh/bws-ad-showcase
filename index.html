Process

How the three slides went from brief to finished image.

  1. 01

    Research

    Pulled sample briefs and audited how other fitness and supplement brands format a hook → insight → takeaway carousel.

  2. 02

    Narrow down

    Shortlisted the directions that matched the brand voice and the brief, and dropped the rest.

  3. 03

    Personalize & sketch

    Sketched the idea: let each slide's central visual literally hold the food its message is about.

  4. 04

    Find the elements

    Sourced the food photography, glass-vessel references, icons, and BWS type system the sketch needed.

  5. 05

    Build in Illustrator

    Laid out typography, logo, color, and grid for every slide in Adobe Illustrator.

  6. 06

    AI-generated composition

    Generated and tested the food-filled vessel composites with AI, refining the prompt until the composition and lighting matched the brief.

Recommendations

Instagram page improvement ideas for @builtwithscience — tap a card to expand.

Built With Science already has a recognizable visual identity: bold typography, blue/white/black color blocking, fitness imagery, and science-backed messaging. However, some covers feel visually crowded because the headline, image, data, and supporting text compete for attention at the same time.

I would recommend creating a more consistent cover system with a clearer hierarchy: one dominant hook, one strong visual, and minimal supporting text. For example, each cover could follow a tighter structure: bold headline at the top or left, one main image or diagram as the visual anchor, and a small brand marker in a fixed position.

Expected outcome: This would make the grid easier to scan, improve first-slide readability, and increase swipe-through because users can understand the topic instantly before deciding to engage.

The page does a strong job using research-based topics, but some scientific content could be simplified more visually. When posts include studies, data, or comparisons, I would suggest building each carousel around a simple narrative: problem, evidence, takeaway.

For example, instead of showing a complex scatter plot or dense study information immediately, the design could first introduce the question, then show a simplified visual comparison, then end with a practical rule the audience can apply. Charts should feel like part of the story, not like screenshots from a paper. Small annotations, highlighted zones, and simplified labels would help make the science easier to digest.

Expected outcome: This would protect the scientific credibility of the brand while making the content more accessible. It would likely improve saves and shares because users would better understand the information without needing to read the caption.

The current page is strong, but many posts rely on similar visual patterns: big type, blue backgrounds, fitness photos, and comparison-style layouts. This works, but over time the grid can feel slightly repetitive. I would recommend developing a few repeatable content formats that each serve a different purpose, for example:

  • Myth vs. Reality — posts for high-share topics
  • Quick Calculator — posts for practical fitness/nutrition guidance
  • Study Breakdown — posts for scientific credibility
  • Mistake/Fix — posts for exercise education
  • Meme-style — posts for reach and relatability

Each format could still use the same brand colors and typography, but with a slightly different layout system so the page feels more dynamic.

Expected outcome: This would create more variety while keeping brand consistency. It would also help the audience recognize content types faster, improve retention across the page, and give the team clearer templates for scaling content production.

Overall, I think the biggest opportunity is not changing the brand direction, but refining the system: clearer covers, more digestible science visuals, and stronger repeatable content formats that make the page feel both more premium and easier to engage with.
